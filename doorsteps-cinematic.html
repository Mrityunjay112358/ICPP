OUR STORY

I was ten when I first understood how unequal access to education could be.

A woman who worked in our home brought her daughter with her — a girl my age. I had just come back from school, talking about what I had learned, while she had come to work. When I asked why she wasn't in school, the answer was simple: NO TIME, NO MONEY, and TOO LATE

That contrast stayed with me.

So I started small. I began teaching her whatever I knew, sitting together each day after she finished her work. Over time, something shifted — not just in what she learned, but in what she believed was possible. She began to want school for herself.

That moment grew.

But as the effort expanded, it became clear that teaching alone wasn't enough. Children weren't out of school simply because they hadn't learned — they were held back by financial pressure, unsupported systems, and the belief that they had fallen too far behind.

The Doorsteps Project was built from that realisation.

Not just to teach, but to remove the barriers around learning — so that returning to school becomes possible, sustainable, and lasting.

Our Approach

A Closed-Loop Model for
Education Reintegration

We don't address one barrier at a time — we remove every barrier that keeps a child out of school.

01

Identify

We work within underserved communities to identify children who are out of school — often due to financial pressure, migration, or prolonged absence.

02

Bridge Learning

Children attend daily doorstep learning sessions focused on literacy, numeracy, and life skills.

These programmes are designed for students who have been out of school for 1–3 years, helping them rebuild confidence and academic readiness.

03

Family Support

We work directly with families to address the root economic causes of school dropout.

This includes linking households to government welfare and income-support schemes, ensuring that education does not come at the cost of survival.

04

School Reintegration

Through partnerships with government schools and organisations, we create guaranteed pathways for children to re-enter formal education.

Students are placed into appropriate grade levels with support systems already in place.

05

Retention & Mentorship

Reintegration is not the end.

We provide continued mentorship, monitor attendance, and conduct sessions on mental health, resilience, and peer support — ensuring children stay in school and continue to progress.

06

System Strengthening

We also strengthen the ecosystem around the child by:

  • Supporting partner schools
  • Training youth volunteers
  • Integrating child-rights awareness into learning

This ensures long-term, sustainable impact beyond individual reintegration.

This model ensures that children don't just return to school — they remain there, supported by systems designed for their success.

Worldwide Impact

Global Success

While the mission remains constant — ensuring every child returns to school — the barriers are not the same everywhere.

The Doorsteps Project adapts to local realities, addressing the root causes that keep children out of education across different regions.

In India

🇮🇳

The Doorsteps Project began in India, working within informal worker communities where children are often pushed out of school due to financial pressure.

Here, the foundation of the model was built:

  • Bridge learning programmes across 15 districts, preparing out-of-school children to return to classrooms
  • Direct family engagement, linking 5,000+ households to government welfare and income-support schemes
  • Structured reintegration through partnerships with government schools and organisations like Ekal Vidyalaya and Friends of Tribal Society

Alongside this, Ariana contributed as part of Students For LAST-MILE Learning, supporting large-scale fundraising and school development efforts that strengthened over 550 rural schools — ensuring reintegrated students entered functional learning environments.

The initiative also led one of the largest child-rights legal awareness efforts, earning an Asia Record for the largest legal rights awareness campaign, expanding legal literacy across communities and schools.

This phase established a key principle:
Education access fails when survival is at risk.

In Africa

🌍

As the model expanded, a new barrier became clear.

In flood-prone regions such as Lagos, students are not out of school because of cost —
they simply cannot reach it.

The model adapted.

Through the Bridges to Blocks (Itowolo Infrastructure Project):

  • Unsafe wooden crossings are being replaced with permanent, flood-resilient infrastructure
  • Safe, reliable access to education is being restored

This is supported by collaboration with the State Universal Basic Education Board, aligning implementation with public education systems and enabling long-term scalability.

Alongside infrastructure, the project integrates:

  • Climate literacy programmes across secondary schools
  • Student-led environmental action and policy awareness

This phase reinforced:
Without physical access, education cannot exist.

In Nepal

🇳🇵

In Nepal, the barrier is not just access — but disruption.

Children affected by conflict and displacement face interrupted education, instability, and emotional stress that directly impact their ability to learn.

Here, the model evolved further.

The Doorsteps Project prioritises these children by:

  • Delivering bridge learning programmes tailored for disrupted education journeys
  • Embedding structured mental health and resilience sessions within learning environments
  • Working closely with government schools to ensure supported reintegration

The focus is not just returning children to classrooms —
but ensuring they are able to rebuild confidence, stability, and continuity in learning.

This phase highlighted:
Education must also rebuild what instability takes away.

Across all regions, the barriers differ —
but the response remains intentional and interconnected.
